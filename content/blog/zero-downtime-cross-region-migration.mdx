Migrating a live production application across AWS regions is the kind of
project where "it mostly worked" isn't good enough. Here's roughly how it
went.

## Start with the data, not the compute

The instinct is to think about servers first. In practice, the hard part is
always data — replication lag, consistency guarantees, and the cutover
window. We stood up read replicas in the target region well before touching
any traffic routing, and watched replication lag under real load for days
before trusting it.

## Shift traffic gradually, not all at once

Instead of a hard cutover, DNS weighting shifted a small percentage of
traffic to the new region first. That gave us a way to catch
region-specific issues — latency to a dependency, a misconfigured security
group, an IAM role that existed in one region but not the other — while the
blast radius was still small.

## Watch the boring metrics

The dashboards that mattered most during the migration weren't
exotic — they were basic error rates, p99 latency, and connection pool
saturation. Nothing dramatic; just enough signal to notice a problem within
minutes instead of hearing about it from a customer.

## Result

The cutover finished with zero customer-visible downtime. The unglamorous
truth is that it worked because of the boring parts: staged rollout,
health checks at every stage, and a rollback plan we hoped not to use.
